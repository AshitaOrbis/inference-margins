The strongest frontier-scale public anchor I found is Qwen3-Coder-480B-A35B-Instruct-FP8 on four Ironwood chips:

  • 1,024 input / 8,192 output workload
  • 2,075.47 aggregate output tokens/s, or 518.86 tokens/s/chip
  • Current four-chip on-demand list price: $48/hour
  • Derived accelerator-rental floor: $6.42 per million output tokens
  • At the current three-year commitment price: $2.89 per million output tokens

That is a real named-model, named-precision, named-topology serving benchmark. It is nevertheless a maximum-throughput vendor reference run at infinite offered load, not a production cost disclosure or a latency-constrained serving result. [cite: turn358674view0, turn130469view1, turn583429view0]

The rigorous negative finding is narrower but important:

There is no public basis for calculating Gemini gross margin from TPU economics. Public TPU rental prices are external customer list prices, not Google’s internal TPU fleet cost, and Google has not published a mapping from a named Gemini API SKU to TPU generation, topology, precision, output throughput, traffic mix, utilization, or supporting-model overhead.

Calculation convention

For a benchmark reporting aggregate output-token throughput, I use:

[ \text{Accelerator $ per 1M output tokens}

\frac{ N_{\text{chips}}\times $/{\text{chip-hour}} }{ \text{output tokens/sec}\times 3600 } \times 10^6 ]

This is an accelerator-rental floor, not fully loaded serving COGS. It excludes:

  • Latency/SLO headroom and replica redundancy
  • Idle time and utilization losses
  • Host CPU, networking, storage, GKE, load balancing, and observability
  • Compilation, model loading, failover, and maintenance
  • Ancillary models, safety classifiers, routers, retrieval, and speculative-draft models
  • Engineering, support, and datacenter overhead beyond what is embedded in the cloud rental price

Where a benchmark has a specified input length, its reported output-token rate implicitly includes the cost of processing those inputs. It should therefore be read as cost per output token for that particular input/output workload, not as a universal decode-only cost.

All calculated prices below use the current lowest listed US price for each generation as of July 15, 2026. Pairing a current price with an older benchmark is a current repricing of the benchmark, not necessarily the economics prevailing when it was published.

1. July 15, 2026 — Public price legs exist for all three generations

Claim. GCP publishes stable on-demand, DWS Flex, DWS Calendar, one-year, and three-year per-chip-hour rates for v5e, v6e, and Ironwood. Ironwood is currently $12/chip-hour on demand; v6e is $2.70; v5e is $1.20. Three-year rates are $5.40, $1.22, and $0.54 respectively.

Source quality. Official commercial price card; strongest possible source for external list-price economics.

Quantitative-anchor status. Yes, for the $/hour leg. It does not disclose Google’s internal fleet cost. Spot remains a dynamic price rather than a stable published number. [cite: turn358674view0]

2. April 10, 2024 — JetStream supplied a clean v5e INT8 throughput-and-cost anchor

Claim. Google reported JetStream on a v5e-8 slice with continuous batching and INT8 weights, activations, and KV cache. With maximum input and output lengths of 1,024 tokens, it reported:

  • Llama 2 13B: 3,938 output tok/s
  • Gemma 7B: 4,030 output tok/s
  • Llama 2 7B: 4,783 output tok/s

The original publication also gave an explicit three-year-CUD estimate of approximately $0.30 per million output tokens for Gemma 7B and Llama 2 13B and $0.25 for Llama 2 7B.

Primary source and date.
https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/compute/accelerating-ai-inference-with-google-cloud-tpus-and-gpus — 2024-04-10

Source quality. Vendor benchmark. The workload, slice size, continuous-batching mode, and quantization are unusually well specified. It is not independently reproduced and represents a maximum-throughput operating point.

Quantitative-anchor status. Yes. Repricing Llama 2 13B at today’s rates gives $0.677/M output tokens on demand and $0.305/M under the current three-year rate. This is probably the cleanest v5e anchor, although not a frontier-size model. [cite: turn291446view0, turn551217view0, turn551217view1]

3. July 26, 2024 — Hex-LLM supplied the strongest public v5e 70B anchor

Claim. Google reported Hex-LLM on eight v5e chips using a ShareGPT variable-length workload. At its highest-throughput point:

  • Gemma 7B reached 6,250 output tok/s.
  • INT8 weight-quantized Llama 2 70B reached 1,510 output tok/s.

Google separately reported low-load per-output-token latencies, but the peak-throughput and minimum-latency figures are different points on the load curve and must not be combined into one serving configuration.

Primary source and date.
https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/ai-machine-learning/hex-llm-on-tpus-in-vertex-ai-model-garden/ — 2024-07-26

Source quality. Vendor benchmark with a realistic variable-length dataset and a named 70B model. The source specifies INT8 weight quantization but does not fully specify activation and KV-cache precision.

Quantitative-anchor status. Yes. At current list pricing, 1,510 tok/s on a $9.60/hour v5e-8 slice implies:

  • On demand: $1.766/M output tokens
  • DWS Flex: $0.883/M
  • Three-year: $0.795/M

This is the best simple public v5e anchor for a large dense model. [cite: turn737569view5, turn737569view6, turn737569view7]

7. April 16, 2026 — Google’s RPA paper supplies exact BF16 v6e rates, but as a software-progress figure

Claim. A Google-authored paper on Ragged Paged Attention reports up to 86% memory-bandwidth utilization in decode and 73% model-FLOP utilization in prefill on Ironwood. A fixed-workload software-progress figure also reports, for 1,800 input and 128 output tokens in BF16:

  • Llama 3 8B on v6e-1: 11.6 requests/s as of August 14, 2025
  • Llama 3 70B on v6e-8: 7.65 requests/s as of August 14, 2025

Because every request has 128 output tokens, these imply:

  • 8B: 1,484.8 output tok/s on one chip
  • 70B: 979.2 output tok/s on eight chips

Primary source and date.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15464 — submitted 2026-04-16

Source quality. Vendor-authored research preprint. Precision, model, topology, and fixed output length are specified; the chart is primarily a history of software improvements rather than a complete latency-qualified serving benchmark.

Quantitative-anchor status. Yes, as a conservative technical anchor. Current on-demand accelerator floors are approximately $0.505/M for the 8B run and $6.13/M for the 70B run. The large difference from the 2025 JetStream 70B figure illustrates how strongly TPU $/token depends on serving engine, quantization, batching, and benchmark workload. [cite: turn851529view0, turn851529view1, turn630152view1]

The 86%/73% utilization results are not themselves cost anchors because they do not disclose end-to-end tokens/s for the corresponding Ironwood runs. [cite: turn851529view4]

Power and TCO comparison verdict

What is public

Google publishes generation-to-generation peak/specification claims:

  • Trillium: over 67% greater energy efficiency than v5e.
  • Ironwood: approximately twice Trillium’s performance per watt.
  • Ironwood’s cited comparison is peak FP8 FLOPS divided by TDP, not LLM serving tokens/J. [cite: turn130789view0, turn130789view1]

Google’s v5e and v6e spec pages also expose a relative “expected performance/TCO” index, with v5e shown below v6e, but the index is not tied to a named model, serving workload, price basis, latency target, or disclosed methodology. It is not usable as a quantitative serving-TCO anchor. [cite: turn763260view0, turn846552view0]

The independent Gemma 4 study provides a useful performance-per-rental-dollar comparison against two H100s, especially at long context, but it does not measure wall power. [cite: turn632373view2, turn632373view3]

Rigorous negative finding

I found no public, credible, apples-to-apples benchmark that simultaneously provides:

  • The same named LLM
  • The same model and KV-cache precision
  • The same input/output distribution
  • Comparable TTFT/TPOT or another latency SLO
  • Measured wall or accelerator power
  • TPU v5e, v6e, or v7 versus H200 or GB200

Therefore:

  • There is a public TPU-versus-H100 performance/$ anchor.
  • There is no comparable public TPU-versus-H200/GB200 measured tokens/J anchor.
  • Google’s Ironwood and Trillium perf/W numbers should remain classified as vendor peak/spec claims, not serving measurements.
  • Any modeled TPU versus GB200 energy advantage would presently be assumption-driven.

Gemini serving-cost and margin verdict

A valid model-level API margin calculation would require:

[ \text{Gross margin}

1- \frac{\text{fully loaded serving COGS per token}} {\text{API list revenue per token}} ]

Even the narrower accelerator-only approximation,

[ 1- \frac{\text{TPU rental floor per output token}} {\text{Gemini API output list price}}, ]

is not defensible because the numerator and denominator would refer to different things:

  1. The public TPU benchmarks are predominantly Llama, Gemma, Qwen, and Mixtral self-hosting runs.
  2. Google does not publicly map a named Gemini API SKU to a TPU generation, chip count, precision, serving engine, or measured output tokens/s.
  3. GCP’s TPU rental price is the customer-facing retail price, not Google’s internal depreciation, power, networking, or datacenter cost.
  4. Gemini production includes routers and supporting models that are absent from the open-model accelerator benchmarks.
  5. API traffic has a latency and utilization profile that is not represented by request-rate=inf saturation tests.

Accordingly:

No rigorous public Gemini cost-per-token or list-price gross-margin anchor exists.

The production 0.24 Wh/prompt disclosure is valuable, and JetStream’s use in Gemini serving establishes technological relevance, but neither closes the missing token, model, topology, utilization, and dollar-cost fields. [cite: turn737569view1, turn495624view1]

Recommended modeling treatment

For a quantitative serving-economics model, I would represent TPU with three separate cases rather than one point estimate:

1. Saturation accelerator floor

Use the published output throughput and current per-chip rental rate directly. This captures the best case at effectively full utilization.

Best examples:

  • v5e Llama 2 70B: $1.77/M on demand
  • v6e Llama 2 70B: $0.94/M on demand, with a major precision caveat
  • v7 Qwen3-Coder-480B-A35B: $6.42/M on demand
  • v7 Qwen3.5-397B-A17B: $3.37/M on demand

2. Commitment-price accelerator floor

Use DWS Flex or a three-year commitment according to workload interruptibility and capacity certainty. Do not describe DWS as Spot.

Examples:

  • Ironwood Qwen3-Coder: $3.21/M DWS, $2.89/M three-year
  • v6e Qwen3-32B: $1.07/M DWS, $0.97/M three-year
  • v5e Llama 2 70B: $0.88/M DWS, $0.79/M three-year

3. Production-adjusted scenario

Apply explicit, separately modeled multipliers for:

  • Achieved utilization
  • Latency/SLO headroom
  • Redundancy and failover
  • Host/infrastructure overhead
  • Ancillary model overhead
  • Effective price versus public cloud list price

For example, if a service realizes only 40% of saturation throughput after SLO headroom and utilization, the accelerator component is 2.5 times the benchmark floor before adding non-accelerator costs. This should be an explicit scenario assumption, not silently embedded in the TPU benchmark.

The public evidence supports the accelerator-floor cases. It does not identify a single empirically justified production-utilization multiplier for Gemini.
